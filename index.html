PRIVACY FIRST
The privacy promise

Your data. Your phone. Your business.

Privacy isn't a footnote — it's the architecture. Three non-negotiables, on by default, no setting to turn them off because there's nothing to turn on.

Stays on your phone

No Milely server. No analytics. No telemetry. No account to create. The app declares zero data collection in its App Privacy report. The only outbound network call is the one you trigger when you save a backup to your own cloud folder.

Backed up to a folder you pick

iCloud Drive, Google Drive, Dropbox, OneDrive, Box — pick once via the iOS Files picker and Milely writes a JSON snapshot into a Milely Backups sub-folder you control. Or skip cloud entirely and keep manual JSON exports on the device.

Yours to delete, any time

Long-press a trip and tap Delete, or uninstall to wipe everything. The trip is gone from the only place it ever existed — there is no server-side copy to "request deletion" of.

Architecture, not policy

We don't store or sell your driving data.

The big mileage apps store your trip data on their servers. Their privacy policies typically allow them to aggregate it, share it with partners, or use it to train models. That's not a violation — it's how cloud-based SaaS works. The company is the customer; the driver is the data.

Milely is built the other way around. Your trips live on your iPhone. We don't run a server they could be uploaded to. Even if we wanted to sell or share your driving data, we couldn't — we never have it. That's not a policy promise; it's an architectural fact.

Backups go to your iCloud, your Google Drive, your Dropbox — never to us. The only copy of your trip log that ever exists outside your phone is the audit-ready PDF you choose to produce at year-end.

Smart Suggestions

Milely learns from you. On your phone.

The third time you classify a trip from your office to Acme Realty's listing as "showing," Milely starts pre-filling that purpose for you.

The pattern-learning model trains locally on your device against your own past classifications — using Apple's standard on-device language tools, the same kind that power iPhone autocorrect and Siri suggestions. No Milely account. No Milely server. There can't be a leak because the training data never leaves your phone.

Include or exclude — never gray

Every trip is either in your report, or kept aside.

No "to-do" pile. No half-classified rows haunting your dashboard. Every trip Milely records is either part of your annual report or kept aside for your records — and you can flip between the two with one swipe.

Swipe right in Logs to Include a trip in your annual report. Swipe left to Exclude it (gym runs, school pickups, the Costco trip). Excluded trips stay in your records, but they're kept out of every deduction total — Reports, Dashboard, exports.

Audit-defensive by design

Make your life boring if anyone ever wants to see.

Five audit-trail features, on by default, no add-on tier:

  • Auto-lock on saveThe moment you save a classified trip, it locks itself — no extra tap, no chance to forget. Every later change is captured in the trip's edit history with a timestamp and the old/new value.
  • Audit Lock (automatic)On January 31 each year, the prior year's trips lock automatically so you can't accidentally edit them mid-audit. A year-end-lock banner appears in the Reports tab so you can see the year is sealed at a glance. Unlocking any individual trip is a deliberate two-tap action that writes a timestamped event to the trip's edit history.
  • Per-trip edit historyEvery field-level change records a TripEditEvent with timestamp, old value, and new value — visible from the trip's detail view, so the full timeline is always there if your CPA asks how a record evolved.
  • Trip recovery if iOS reclaims memory mid-driveIf your phone runs out of memory or the app force-quits mid-trip, you'll see a Recover trip in progress? prompt the next time you open Milely. Your route, distance, and time so far are preserved — nothing is lost.
  • Year-end odometer reconciliationBeginning- and end-of-year readings per vehicle, reconciled against actual miles logged. Printed on the cover page of the annual PDF report.

Built for the way drivers actually work.

You don't classify trips at your desk — you classify them at a stoplight, between showings, on the way to the next job. Milely's smaller features are built for that.

Three trip-recording modes

Settings → Trip Settings splits into Manual (tap Start, drive, tap End), Automatic (motion + significant-location-changes wake the recorder when you start driving), and Universal (any active recording responds to CarPlay/Bluetooth disconnects, pause-on-disconnect rules, and the overnight cutoff). Pick the mode that fits your week.

Smart Suggestions, on-device

The third time you classify a trip from your office to Acme Realty as showing, Milely starts pre-filling that combination. The pattern-learning model trains locally with Apple's CoreML and never leaves your phone. Uninstall the app and the model goes with it.

Include or exclude — never gray

Every trip Milely records is either part of your annual report or kept aside. Swipe right in Logs to Include; swipe left to Exclude. Excluded trips stay searchable but are kept out of every deduction total — Reports, Dashboard, exports.

Receipt scanning

Apple's VisionKit document scanner — edge detection, perspective correction, multi-page. EXIF metadata (location, timestamp, device model) is stripped at attach. Up to ten receipts per trip, 5 MB each. Receipts surface beside the trip and on the annual PDF.

Switching from MileIQ, Everlance, or TripLog?

Drop in your CSV — Milely auto-detects the source format from the file's header row, previews the trips, and brings every one with you. Settings → Backup → Import. Your history goes with you so you don't lose deductible miles to "I'll set it up later."

Tax-bracket savings ticker

Set your federal bracket once in Settings → Tax Bracket. Every business mile becomes a real dollar at your bracket — not a generic deduction line, the actual tax saved. The Dashboard ticker turns "I drove 14 miles" into "I just saved $3.24." Quietly motivating.

Project & client tagging

Within a Business, tag each trip with a project — 1428 Maple listing, Smith matter, Dallas job. Surfaces on the CSV and the PDF so you can sort, filter, or pivot when you're billing or reconciling.

Calendar past-trip recovery

Forgot to use the app for three weeks? Settings → Integrations → Recover from Calendar reads recent calendar events with locations — on-device, never transmitted — dedupes against trips you already saved, and proposes the rest.

Weekly review reminder

Pick a day in Settings → Notifications. At 6 PM that day, if you have trips waiting on Include/Exclude, Milely pings you. If everything's reviewed, it stays silent. Five minutes a week.

Backups, your folder

Choose Cloud Folder once via the iOS Files picker — iCloud Drive, Google Drive, Dropbox, OneDrive, or Box — and Milely writes milely-<timestamp>.json snapshots into it. Or pick This iPhone Only and use Manual Export when you want a one-off JSON via the share sheet.

Exports for every accountant

PDF — branded for subscribers, plain for free. Excel (.xls) — opens in Excel, Numbers, Sheets, LibreOffice. CSV — Milely (full) with every column. CSV — QuickBooks Online — drop-in mileage importer. JSON — same shape as the backup snapshot.

While a trip records, your watch shows live miles + duration, the home-screen widget shows month-to-date deduction, and the Lock Screen / Dynamic Island carries the active trip with miles, duration, and deduction. The watch is display-only — recording starts on the phone.
